Coronavirus

Minnesota is reopening, but falls short of normalcy

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune June 24, 2020

As COVID-19 lockdowns gradually roll back and the aftermath of civil unrest somewhat settles down, Minnesotans are seemingly socially distancing less and are more engaged with summertime activities.

Yet with many summer events canceled for the year and several restrictions still in place, normalcy seems far off, and may not fully return anytime soon.

Vehicular traffic has roared back

After weeks of climbing back toward normal levels, Minnesota's roadways are closer to typical activity levels.

Despite traffic density falling significantly since shelter-in-place orders took effect statewide, Minnesota Department of Transportation daily tracking shows the volume of vehicles now looks more like last year at this time.

Social distancing breaks down

On the whole, Minnesotans are back to typical isolation levels after slacking off for a while. Unacast data measuring social distancing shows Minnesota's grade had been sliding gradually from a B when shelter-in-place started in March and by May was scoring an F, like much of the United States.

And Google data shows where they're going: mostly to parks and other recreation areas to take advantage of Minnesota's limited summer weather.

Buses and trains remain emptier

Even though transit routes have started opening back up, safety restrictions and service reductions remain across the Twin Cities metro area. That, combined with continued work-from-home arrangements, seem to still be depressing ridership on buses and light rail.

Ridership ceased completely as Metro Transit shut down over the period of unrest and mandatory curfews enforced immediately after the death of George Floyd, but have bounced back.

Restaurant diners are returning, slowly

Bars and restaurants may be missing typical crowds for a while, even with the reopening of indoor and outdoor dining. Concerns over safety, restrictions on restaurant crowd capacity, diner spacing and required reservations may be keeping many would-be patrons away.
